BASICS - RULES

Cricket is played between two teams of 11 players.

The game of cricket is typically divided into two halves - the first innings and the second innings. In the first innings, one team bats whilst the other team bowls and fields. In the second innings, the teams swap and the batting team now bowls/fields while the fielding team gets to bat.

The aim of the batting team is to gather as many runs as they can so that the opposite team will not be able to beat their score. Two batters will start the game batting and as one batter gets out, another will come in to bat until the batting team runs out of players or the overs are completed. Batters a range of techniques which allows them to score more runs in specific areas of the field or to combat good bowlers.

The aim of the bowling/fielding team is to stop the batting team from scoring more runs than their team. This is typically done by taking wickets which means getting the batters out. However there is a range of tactics used by the bowling and fielding team to put pressure on the batters and prevent them from scoring runs. There are 11 ways to get a batter out, here are the most common:

  1. Bowled: The bowler's ball hits the batsmens wickets and the bails fall off. This is one of the most common ways batters get out.

  2. Caught: The batsmen hits the ball in the air and is caught by a fielder on the full (caught before it hits the ground). There are three ways to get caught:

    • Caught by fielder: A fielder catches the ball on the full, off the bat.

    • Caught by wicketkeeper: The ball will usually have a small nick on the bat resulting in it carrying to the wicketkeeper behind the wickets.

    • Caught and bowled: The ball is hit back to the bowler on the full and the bowler who bowled takes the catch

  3. Stumped: If the batter steps out of crease and misses the ball, the wicketkeeper may dislodge the bails on the wickets resulting in the batter being stumped.

  4. Hit Wicket: If the batter accientally falls, steps or hits the wickets resulting in the bails dislodging, it is considered out.

  5. LBW (Leg Before Wicket): The bat may fail to make contact with the ball, resulting in the ball hitting the player on the body. If the opposition believes the ball would be going on to hit the wickets if not for the batter, they can appeal to the umpire who may deem the batter out if they agree.

  6. Run Out: The fielding team can attempt to run the batters out by hitting the wickets or dislodging the bails with the ball before the batter returns to their crease.
