BASICS - GLOSSARY

All Rounder: A player whose strength is both batting and bowling.

Appeal: When the bowling and fielding team believe the batter is out, they will often turn to the umpire and yell "Howzat" or quite simply cheering until the umpire makes a decision. The is not usually relevant when the batter is bowled or clearly caught out.

Around the wicket: A right handed bowler running past the right side of the non-strikers wickets and vice versa for a left handed bowler.

The Ashes: The Test series played between England and Australia. According to Wikipedia and the Wisden Dictionary of Cricket "The Ashes originated as a result of a satirical obituary published in a British newspaper, The Sporting Times, in 1882 after a match at The Oval in which Australia beat England on an English ground for the first time. The obituary stated that English cricket had died, and the body would be cremated and the ashes taken to Australia. The English press dubbed the next English tour to Australia (1882â€“83) as the quest to regain The Ashes. During that tour a small terracotta urn was presented to England captain Ivo Bligh by a group of Melbourne women. The urn is reputed to contain the ashes of one or two bails."

Asking Rate: The run rate rate required for the batting team in the second innings to equal or beat the other teams score (in a limited overs game).

Attacking shot: An aggressive shot played with the purpose of scoring runs.

Back Foot Shot: A shot played by the batter with most of their body weight on the back foot.

Bails: The two wooden pieces which sit on top of the wickets.

Batting Order: The order in which the batters in each team bat - in order of openers, top order, middle order and lower order.

Beamer: A ball that is bowled on the full (without bouncing) and is above the batters waist. This is considered an illegal delivery and a no-ball.

Block: To block a ball is a defensive shot.

Bouncer: A ball bowled at high speed which rises at or above the batters head after bouncing.

Boundary: A four or a six, or the perimeter of the ground.

Bye: When runs are scored when the batter makes no contact with the ball and the ball travels past the wicketkeeper without them stopping the ball.

Century: When a batter scores 100 runs.

Crease: A line marked on the ground near the stumps

Dead Ball: The period between deliveries in which the batter may not be given out or score any runs.

Death Bowler: A bowler who usually bowls in the last overs of an innings. Death bowlers usually put pressure on the batters and are particularly hard to score of.

Death Overs: Last few overs of a limited overs game. If the batting team has wickets in hand, they usually play agressively and score a lot of runs.

DRS (Decision Review System): The system usually used in domestic, franchise and international cricket to review the umpires decision if believed the wrong decision is made.

Declaration: When the captain of a side voluntarily ends their teams batting innings because they believe they have enough runs to secure the game.

Delivery: The act of bowling a ball.

Diamond Duck: A better being run out at the non-strikers end without facing a delivery or the opening batter getting out first ball of the game.

Dismissal: To get the batter out.

Direct Hit: A throw from the fielder which directly hits the wickets.

Doosra: A delivery bowled as an off-break but turns the wrong way. This confuses the batter.

Dot Ball: A delivery which the batting team scores no runs off.

Down the Wicket: When the batter moves down the pitch in an attempt to play the ball on the half-volley.

Drinks: Both teams take a short break during the innings for a drinks break.

Drive: To play the ball straight with a straight bat, usually between long off to cover.

Duck: When the batter gets out without scoring a run.

Edge: When there is a slight deviation of the ball from off the bat.

Fast Bowling: A type of bowling which the ball is delivered at high pace.

Flight: A delivery which is bowled by a spinner and loops up.

Flipper: A delivery bowled by a spinner which has under-spin, causing it to bounce lower than usual.

Follow through The actions of a bowlers body after the release of the ball or the batter after having hit the ball.

Forward Defence: A front foot defensive shot.

Four: A shot that reaches the boundary after touching the ground which gives four runs to the batting side.

Free Hit: A penalty given to the batter after facing a no-ball, allowing them to get a free delivery in which they can not get out, with the exception of being run out.

French Cut: A badly executed shot resulting in the ball being edged and narrowly missing the wickets.

Full Toss: A delivery which is bowled at the batter on the full.

Golden Duck: When the batter gets out on the first ball they face.

Guard: Where the individual batter bats in relation to the stumps. The batter often marks a line on the pitch to signal where they want to bat in relation to the stumps.

Half Century: When the batter gets 50 runs.

Hat-trick: When the bowlers gets three wickets in three consecutive balls.

Hot Spot: Infrared technology used by the umpires to determine whether the ball has made contact with the bat. Usually used for determining if the batter is caught out or out LBW.

Inswing: A delivery which moves into the batter, from off to leg or middle stump.

Innings: A player or teams turn to bowl or bat.

Jaffa: A very well bowled ball, usually considered "unplayable."

Knuckle Ball: A delivery which is bowled with the ball gripped by the bowlers knuckles. Usually a slower ball.

LBW: Leg before wicket. When the ball has hit the batter body and is believed to be travelling on to hit the wickets, if not for batter making contact with their body.

Leave: When the batter does not attempt to play the ball and decides to let the ball travel to the keeper without offering a shot. Usually a good ball that is not hitting the wickets would be left.

Leg Break: A delivery which is bowled by a spinner and moves from the leg side to the off side.

Leg Side: A rear side of the field when the batter is in their stance.

Leggie: A Leg Spin bowler.

Length: The position on the pitch where the ball bounces. Short, good, full etc.

Line: The position on the pitch where the ball bounces in relation the the stumps.

Lower Order: The batters who bat between 8-11. Usually less skilled with batting and more proficient with either bowling or wicketkeeping.

Maiden Over: An over bowled without conceeding any runs.

Mankad: A controversial way of getting the non-striking batter out through the bowler discontinuing the delivery in an attempt to run the non-striker out while they are backing up.

Middle Order: Batters who bat from 5 to 7 in the batting order.

Nets: A pitch surrounded by net from three sides which allows batters and bowlers to train.

Nightwatchman: A lower order batter sent out to bat when the light is bad to play out the overs until the end of that day to protect higher order batters for the next day of play.

No Ball: An illegal delivery which awards the batting team with a run and is re-bowled with a free hit.
