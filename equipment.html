BASICS - EQUIPMENT

Cricket often requires a lot of equipment and gear. It is helpful to learn what gear is required and why.

Basic Equipment List

  • Ball: There are also three different game balls which can be used. Red balls are used for most Test Matches played at day, as they are easy to pick up against the white uniforms and also in the sky. Pink balls are used in Test Matches as well, but are used for Day Night games. This means the game is played during the day and into the night. Pink balls are used for these matches as they are easy to see against the white uniforms and also easy to see in the night sky. White balls are used in limited over games (T20S and ODIS) as they are easily seen against the coloured uniforms as well as in the sky. For training purposes, any ball will do but it is important to find out which ball you will playing with during games.

  • Wickets: Wickets are placed at both ends of the pitch. Generally, wooden wickets are used in competitive games, while metal stumps are used at trainings.

  • Bat: There are many different bats available. The key to a good bat is ensuring that it is the right height and weight for you personally. Different people also have different preferences in regards to bat weights and brands. Some people prefer heavy bats to assist with powering the ball, while others prefer light bats which allow them to have more control.

  • Helmet: Helmets are usually required for batters, wicketkeepers and close in fielders. Hemlets are required to protect your head and face from any potential impacts from the cricket ball. They help prevent serious injury, due the hardness of the cricket ball. It is important to make sure your helmet fits properly and is comfortable. Hemlets should be replaced after taking a significant knock from the cricket ball.

  • Batting Gloves: Batting gloves are worn by the batter to protect their hands from injury. It is important to make sure that the type of Batting Glove you are using is fit for the level of cricket you are playing. Some gloves will have more padding to protect the batter from faster bowling, while others have less padding as less protection is needed as the bowling is not too dangerous.

  • Batting Inners (Optional) : Batting Inners are thin gloves worn inside the batting gloves to reduce sweat. For some batters, they also help increase their grip of the bat.

  • Arm Guard (Optional) : Arm guards are used by some batters to protect their forearm from impact from the cricket ball.

  • Abdominal Guard/Box: Abdominal Guards or Box's are worn under cricket pants to protect groin area.

  • Thigh Guard: Thigh Guards are often worn under cricket pants to protect your thighs from injury from the cricket ball. There are two types of thigh guards - one with two protective paddings (for each thigh) and the other with just one protective padding for the front leg. What type of thigh guard you use is up to you, but it is important to choose a thigh guard which is comfortable and you feel well protected in.

  • Leg Pads: Leg Pads are worn on the outside of your cricket pants to protect your shins and knees from injury. Leg pads need to be large enough to protect this whole section of your leg, however you should also be able to run comfortably in them. This does take practice to get used to.

  • Wicketkeeping Gloves: Wicketkeeping Gloves are worn by wicketkeepers to assist them in catch the ball. They are also protective, and reduce the impact of the cricket ball on the wicketkeepers hands.

  • Wicketkeeping Inners (Optional) : Wicketkeeping Inners are worn by many wicketkeepers to reduce the impact of the cricket ball on their hands. They are thinner than Wicketkeeping Gloves but are padded.

  • Wicketkeeping Pads: Wicketkeeping pads are very similar to batting pads but are generally thinner and lighter. They are worn by wicketkeepers to protect their shins from impact from the cricket ball but are less padded to allow for flexibility needed for wicketkeeping.

  • Cricket Sunglasses: Many fielders, wicketkeepers and sometimes bowlers (generally spinners) wear sunglasses to reduce the glare of the sun on their eyes, and increase their visibility of the ball.

  • Cricket Shoes: There are many different cricket shoes available. However the most widely used cricket shoes have metal or rubber spikes to prevent slipping on the grass and pitch.

Batting Equipment

As a batter, it is important to stay safe and protect yourself by using all the required gear.

  • Bat

  • Hemlet

  • Batting Gloves

  • Batting Inners (Optional)

  • Arm Guard (Optional)

  • Abdominal Guard/Box

  • Thigh Guard

  • Leg Pads

  • Cricket Shoes

Wicketkeeping Equipment

As a wicketkeeper, it is also important to stay safe and protect yourself by using all the required gear.

  • Hemlet

  • Cricket Sunglasses (Optional)

  • Wicketkeeping Gloves

  • Wicketkeeping Inners (Optional)

  • Abdominal Guard/Box

  • Wicketkeeping Pads

  • Cricket Shoes

Bowling and Fielding Equipment

Bowlers and fielders don't require much protective equipment. However there is some equipment that can assist them in their long hours on the field.

  • Cricket Sunglasses (Optional)

  • Helmet (for close in fielders only)

  • Abdominal Guard/Box (For close in fielders only)

  • Cricket Shoes
